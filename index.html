Neuroscience of Salience, Vulnerability, and Psychopathology

MSc Neuropsychology — Maastricht University. Research focus: predictive processing, dopaminergic salience attribution, neuroimaging of cognitive control, sexuality and reward systems in severe mental illness.

Research Identity

My work investigates how hierarchical predictive systems generate internal models of the environment, and how disruptions in precision-weighted inference produce psychopathology. I am particularly interested in schizophrenia spectrum disorders, dopaminergic signaling, sexual reward processing, and environmental modulation of cognitive vulnerability.

Methodologically, I integrate behavioral paradigms, large-sample statistical modeling, and neuroimaging approaches including fNIRS. My research bridges computational theory and clinical neurobiology, aiming to produce mechanistic models rather than descriptive symptom accounts.

Research Portfolio

1. Educational Background and Auditory Environment as Determinants of Working Memory Performance

This mixed-design experimental study (N = 297) investigated whether educational attainment moderates working memory performance under varying auditory environments. Participants completed the Digit Symbol Substitution Test under white noise, unintelligible speech, and silent conditions.

A repeated-measures ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of educational level, F(3, 293) = 12.24, p < .001, η² = .09. University-educated participants consistently outperformed lower education groups. No significant interaction was found between sound condition and education level, F(5.81, 567.42) = 0.60, p = .72.

2. Predictive Processing Disturbances in Psychosis

Psychosis may reflect dysregulated hierarchical inference. Within predictive coding models, dopamine modulates precision weighting of prediction errors. Elevated striatal dopamine synthesis capacity observed in first-episode psychosis suggests abnormal precision assignment to irrelevant stimuli.

Aberrant salience attribution destabilizes belief updating, contributing to delusion formation. Functional imaging studies demonstrate altered connectivity between sensory cortex and default mode networks during hallucinations, supporting overweighted top-down priors.

3. Sexuality, Reward Systems, and Psychopathology

Sexual reward processing is mediated by mesolimbic dopaminergic circuitry. Disruptions in reward valuation mechanisms have been implicated in both affective disorders and schizophrenia. Emerging neuroimaging evidence suggests that sexual minority stress may interact with reward circuitry sensitivity, modulating vulnerability for psychopathology.

This line of research integrates minority stress theory with neurobiological models of salience and reinforcement learning, aiming to clarify how environmental stress interfaces with dopaminergic precision signals.
