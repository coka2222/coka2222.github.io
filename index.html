Experimental Research

Educational Background and Auditory Environment as Determinants of Working Memory Performance

Digit Symbol Substitution Test (DSST) Study

Abstract

This study investigated whether educational background and auditory environment influence working memory performance measured through the Digit Symbol Substitution Test. A total of 297 participants completed the task under three auditory conditions: white noise, unintelligible speech, and silence. It was hypothesized that individuals with university education would demonstrate superior performance independent of auditory context.

Method

A mixed-design approach was used. Sound condition (white noise, unintelligible speech, silence) served as a within-subject factor, while educational level (University, HBO, MBO, Other) served as a between-subject factor. The dependent variable was DSST score.

Participants completed the task under all three auditory conditions in counterbalanced order to control for fatigue and practice effects. Educational attainment was self-reported and categorized into four groups.

Results

A repeated measures between-subjects ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of educational level on DSST performance, F(3, 293) = 12.24, p < .001, η² = .09.

University-educated participants consistently outperformed lower educational groups across all auditory conditions. The interaction between education and sound condition was not significant, F(5.81, 567.42) = 0.60, p = .72, η² = .001, indicating that the educational advantage remained stable regardless of auditory environment.

Discussion

Findings indicate that higher educational attainment is associated with enhanced working memory capacity and processing efficiency. Importantly, this advantage appears resilient to auditory distraction. Environmental modulation did not differentially impair lower education groups beyond baseline differences.

These results contribute to understanding how educational experience may strengthen executive functioning mechanisms and support cognitive stability under varying environmental demands.
